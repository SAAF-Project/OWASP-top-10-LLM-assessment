# Internal Audit Assessment of the Reviewed Document

---

## 1. Summary

The document under review is itself an internal audit assessment of a User Access Management Control governing financial systems. It evaluates the control's coverage of the user access lifecycle (provisioning, periodic review, and de-provisioning) and finds it substantially deficient in both design and documentation. The assessment concludes that significant remediation is required to bring the control to an acceptable standard for audit and regulatory compliance purposes.

---

## 2. Key Findings

- **Well-structured assessment format:** The document follows a logical and professional audit reporting structure (Summary, Key Findings, Gaps, Risks, Recommendations), making it clear and actionable for stakeholders.
- **Thorough gap identification:** The assessment identifies eleven distinct and material gaps, ranging from missing version control to the absence of privileged access management, segregation of duties, and monitoring controls. These are appropriate and well-articulated.
- **Risk ratings are provided:** Each identified risk includes a severity rating (HIGH, MEDIUM-HIGH, MEDIUM), which aids in prioritization and management response.
- **Recommendations are phased and practical:** The remediation roadmap is broken into Immediate (0–30 days), Short-Term (30–90 days), and Longer-Term (90–180 days) actions, which is best practice for driving accountability and tracking progress.
- **Correct identification of the critical de-provisioning flaw:** The 30-day termination window is rightly flagged as a high-severity issue requiring immediate remediation.
- **Alignment with recognized frameworks:** The assessment correctly references COSO, COBIT, SOX, PCI-DSS, and GDPR, grounding its findings in established standards.
- **Clear overall conclusion:** The final assessment statement is unambiguous and appropriately direct about the severity of the issues found.

---

## 3. Gaps & Missing Information

- **Absence of the original source document:** The assessment references an underlying control document but does not include or attach it. Without the source material, a reader cannot independently verify the findings or assess whether any aspects were overlooked.
- **No audit methodology disclosed:** The assessment does not describe the methodology used — was this a design-only review, a walkthrough, or an operating effectiveness test? This distinction materially affects the weight of the conclusions.
- **No testing or evidence referenced:** There is no mention of specific evidence examined (e.g., sample access requests, termination records, prior review documentation). An audit assessment should reference the evidence base supporting each finding.
- **No management response or action plan:** A complete audit report typically includes management's response to each finding, agreed-upon remediation actions, responsible parties, and target completion dates.
- **No rating or scoring rubric:** While severity labels (HIGH, MEDIUM, etc.) are used, the criteria for assigning these ratings are not defined. A risk-rating framework or matrix should be disclosed for consistency and transparency.
- **No scope or objectives statement for the audit itself:** The document does not define the audit's scope boundaries, period under review, or specific objectives beyond the implicit review of the control document.
- **No identification of the auditor(s):** The assessment lacks attribution — who performed the review, who supervised it, and who approved the final report.
- **No distribution list or confidentiality marking:** Audit reports governing financial system access controls should be marked with appropriate confidentiality classifications and intended recipients.
- **No reference to prior audits or findings:** There is no indication of whether this area has been audited before, whether prior findings existed, or whether this represents a recurring issue.
- **No quantification of exposure:** While risks are described qualitatively, there is no attempt to quantify potential financial, operational, or compliance exposure (e.g., number of users affected, number of systems in scope, regulatory penalty ranges).
- **Transfer and role change gap could be expanded:** While correctly identified, the assessment does not discuss the risk of contractors, third-party vendors, or temporary workers, which often present distinct and elevated access risks.

---

## 4. Risks Identified

- **Incomplete audit trail for the assessment itself (MEDIUM-HIGH):** Without documented methodology, evidence references, and auditor identification, this assessment may not withstand scrutiny from external auditors, regulators, or the audit committee.
- **Findings may be challenged without source document (MEDIUM):** The absence of the underlying control document means stakeholders cannot validate the assessment independently, potentially undermining credibility and delaying remediation.
- **Lack of management buy-in risk (MEDIUM-HIGH):** Without a management response section, there is no documented commitment to remediate. Findings without agreed-upon action plans frequently stall.
- **Scope gaps may leave risks unaddressed (MEDIUM):** The assessment does not address third-party or vendor access, service accounts, application-to-application credentials, or remote access scenarios — all of which are common vectors for unauthorized access to financial systems.
- **Inconsistent risk rating application (LOW-MEDIUM):** Without a defined rating methodology, the severity assessments may appear subjective, reducing their effectiveness in driving prioritized remediation.
- **Regulatory examination readiness (HIGH):** If this assessment is intended to support SOX compliance or similar regulatory requirements, its current form lacks the formality, evidence documentation, and completeness that external reviewers would expect.

---

## 5. Recommendations

### Documentation and Formalization
- **Attach or reference the original control document** being assessed so that the report is self-contained and independently verifiable.
- **Add a formal scope and objectives section** defining the audit's boundaries, the period under review, the systems and populations considered, and the specific control objectives being evaluated.
- **Include an audit methodology section** specifying whether this was a design assessment, a walkthrough, an operating effectiveness test, or a combination, and describe the procedures performed.
- **Document the evidence base** by referencing specific artifacts examined (e.g., "reviewed 15 access request emails from Q1–Q3 2024," "examined termination report dated XX/XX/XXXX").
- **Add auditor identification and report dating**, including preparer, reviewer, approval authority, report date, and distribution list.
- **Apply a confidentiality classification** consistent with the organization's information classification policy.

### Risk Assessment Enhancement
- **Define and disclose the risk-rating criteria** used (e.g., a likelihood × impact matrix) so that ratings are transparent, consistent, and defensible.
- **Quantify exposure where possible**, such as the number of active user accounts, number of terminated employees with active access, or count of identified SoD conflicts.
- **Expand scope consideration** to include third-party/vendor access, service accounts, API credentials, and remote access pathways in either this assessment or a planned follow-up.

### Stakeholder Engagement
- **Obtain and document formal management responses** for each finding, including agreement or disagreement, planned remediation actions, responsible owners, and target dates.
- **Present findings to the Audit Committee or equivalent governance body** with a clear summary of the overall control environment health and the remediation timeline.
- **Establish a follow-up audit** with a defined date (recommend within 90–120 days of management's agreed remediation deadline) to verify that corrective actions have been implemented and are operating effectively.

### Process Integration
- **Link findings to the organization's risk register** to ensure that identified risks are tracked and monitored at the enterprise level.
- **Reference prior audit history** to establish whether these are new findings or repeat issues, which materially affects the urgency and governance escalation required.
- **Align the remediation timeline with any external audit or regulatory examination schedules** to ensure that critical gaps are addressed before they are independently identified by external parties.

---

**Overall Assessment of this Audit Document:** The assessment demonstrates strong analytical rigor and identifies material control deficiencies with well-prioritized, actionable recommendations. However, as a formal audit deliverable, it lacks essential structural elements — methodology disclosure, evidence documentation, scope definition, auditor attribution, and management responses — that are necessary for it to serve as a complete, defensible audit report. These gaps should be addressed before the report is finalized and issued to stakeholders.
